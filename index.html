Ice Plume Particle Detector

A charged particle detection instrument for spacecraft flying through Enceladus' ice plumes

2023 - 2024
University Research Lab
Team of 4

Project Overview

The completed particle detector assembly

Mission

This project focused on developing and prototyping a charged particle detection instrument designed for spacecraft flying through Enceladus' ice plumes. The detector enables in-situ analysis of charged ice grains to study the subsurface ocean composition.

Challenge

Design a compact, reliable instrument capable of detecting and characterizing charged particles in the harsh space environment while maintaining precise measurements at extremely low temperatures.

Solution

Developed mechanical and electronic subsystems that integrate sensor arrays, signal processing electronics, and thermal management systems into a robust package suitable for deep space missions.

Technologies & Tools

MATLAB LabVIEW C++ PCB Design SolidWorks Manufacturing Signal Processing Thermal Analysis

Design & Development

Electronics Design

Designed custom PCB layouts for sensor signal conditioning, amplification, and digitization. Implemented low-noise circuitry to handle extremely small charge signals from individual particle impacts.

  • Custom charge amplifier circuits with sub-pA noise floors
  • Multi-channel data acquisition system
  • Radiation-hardened component selection
  • Power management for space-grade operation

Mechanical Systems

Developed mechanical housing and sensor mounting structures using SolidWorks. Ensured structural integrity for launch loads while maintaining precise sensor alignment and thermal stability.

  • CAD modeling and FEA structural analysis
  • Thermal management and insulation design
  • Vibration isolation systems
  • Integration with spacecraft interfaces

Software & Data Processing

Implemented real-time data acquisition and processing algorithms in C++ and LabVIEW. Developed MATLAB analysis tools for post-processing and scientific interpretation of particle measurements.

  • Real-time signal processing algorithms
  • Particle event detection and classification
  • Data compression for telemetry
  • Ground support equipment software

Lessons Learned

This project provided invaluable experience in space instrumentation design, particularly in managing the trade-offs between sensitivity, power consumption, and radiation tolerance. The iterative design process taught the importance of early prototyping and comprehensive testing to identify potential issues before final fabrication.

Future Work

The next phase involves miniaturization for reduced mass and power, integration with advanced particle classification algorithms, and qualification testing for flight readiness. Potential applications extend beyond Enceladus to other icy moon missions.
